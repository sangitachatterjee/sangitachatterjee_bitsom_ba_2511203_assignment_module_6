## Part 1: Prompt Design & Iteration

### 1.1 Scenario A: Technical Explanation

Goal: Explain neural networks to a 10-year-old.

**Initial Prompt (V1)**

Explain neural networks to a 10-year-old.

**V1 Output:**

A neural network is a type of computer system inspired by the human brain. It has layers of connected nodes called neurons that process information. When you give it data, each neuron does a small calculation and passes the result to the next layer. After going through many layers, the network produces an output, like recognizing a cat in a photo or predicting a number. Neural networks learn by adjusting the connections between neurons when they make mistakes, similar to how you learn from getting answers wrong on a test.

**Refined Prompt (V2)**

You are a friendly science teacher explaining things to a curious 10-year-old. Explain what a neural network is using a simple real-life analogy. Keep it under 150 words. Don't use words like nodes, layers, or algorithm.

**V2 Output:**

Imagine you and your friends are trying to guess whether a photo shows a dog or a cat. One friend looks at the ears, another looks at the tail, and another looks at the face. Each friend shares what they noticed, and together you make a final guess. If you get it wrong, you talk about what you missed and try again next time.

A neural network works the same way - but inside a computer. Lots of tiny helpers each look at one small piece of information, share their thoughts, and the computer combines everything to make a smart guess. The more it practises, the better it gets!

**Final Prompt (V3)**

You are a science teacher who makes hard topics easy for kids.

A 10-year-old who loves video games asked how computers learn to recognise faces in photos. Explain neural networks using a video game analogy they'd get. Use simple language, give one everyday example besides games, keep it under 120 words, and end with a fun fact. Don't use jargon like neuron, algorithm, gradient, or parameter.

**Final Output:**

Think of a neural network like training a character in a video game. At first your character keeps losing because it doesn't know the rules. But every time it tries, it remembers what worked and what didn't, and gets a little better.

Real computers do this too! When you unlock your phone with your face, the phone has basically practiced on thousands of faces until it knows yours.

Fun fact: the neural networks on your phone are tiny - small enough to fit on a grain of rice - but they're smart enough to recognize you.

## Part 2: Temperature & Parameter Control

### 2.1 Temperature Experimentation

Task I picked: short story opening (2-3 sentences)

ChatGPT free tier doesn't let you change temperature directly, so I followed the assignment note and simulated it by adding different instructions to the same base prompt. Bit of a workaround but it worked fine.

Base prompt (same for all three):

Write a 2-3 sentence opening for a short story about a librarian who discovers a book that wasn't there yesterday. Set it in a small coastal town.

**Low temperature (~0.2)**

Added: Be precise and consistent. Use straightforward language.

Output:

Margaret had worked at the Whitby Public Library for thirty-one years and knew every title on every shelf. This morning, a leather-bound book she had never seen before sat on the returns cart, its cover unmarked and its pages blank. She picked it up, and a single line of text appeared: Return to shelf 14, row 3, before sunset.

**Medium temperature (~0.7)**

No modifier - just the base prompt.

Output:

The tide was still out when Clara unlocked the library, the smell of salt and old paper mixing in the quiet air. On the front desk sat a book with no checkout card, no barcode, and a title she didn't recognize - The Last Chapter of Whitby. She opened it, and the first page was dated tomorrow.

**High temperature (~1.2)**

Added: Be creative and try unexpected ideas. Take risks with the story.

Output:

Elias the librarian stopped mid-yawn when he noticed the book floating three inches above the maritime history section, its pages turning themselves like it was reading its own story before he got the chance. The cover showed a lighthouse that didn't exist on any map of the coast - yet somehow, every patron who walked in today asked for directions to it. He wrote the title in the logbook: Borrowed Time, author unknown, due date: never.

**Comparison**

Low (0.2) - calm and straightforward. Plain language, blank book with instructions on the cover. Very predictable but clear.

Medium (0.7) - bit of mystery. Book titled The Last Chapter of Whitby with first page dated tomorrow. Somewhat surprising but still coherent.

High (1.2) - got weird. Floating self-reading book, phantom lighthouse, patrons all asking about it. Most creative but hard to turn into a normal story from there.

---

### 2.2 Analysis & Recommendations

Low temperature makes sense when you need the same kind of output every time - like pulling fields from a resume into a fixed format, or writing a legal disclaimer where you can't have random creative wording. You'd also use it for summarising a report where accuracy matters more than style.

High temperature is better when you want variety - brainstorming product names, coming up with ad copy, or writing fiction where you want something unexpected. Basically anything where being correct matters less than being interesting.

For my story opening, medium worked best. Low was fine but boring. High was fun but the floating book thing felt hard to build a whole story around. Medium gave me something mysterious (a book dated tomorrow) without going off the rails.

## Part 3: Strategic Prompting Techniques

### 3.1 Chain-of-Thought Prompting

Problem: Option A (train speed)

If a train travels 120 km in 2 hours, then stops for 30 minutes, then travels another 90 km in 1.5 hours, what is its average speed for the entire journey?

**Without chain-of-thought**

Prompt:

If a train travels 120 km in 2 hours, then stops for 30 minutes, then travels another 90 km in 1.5 hours, what is its average speed for the entire journey? Just give me the answer.

Output:

The average speed for the entire journey is 60 km/h.

(This is wrong - correct answer is 52.5 km/h)

**With chain-of-thought**

Prompt:

If a train travels 120 km in 2 hours, then stops for 30 minutes, then travels another 90 km in 1.5 hours, what is its average speed for the entire journey?

Let's solve this step by step. Show your working before the final answer.

Output:

Step 1: Total distance
- First part: 120 km
- Second part: 90 km
- Total = 120 + 90 = 210 km

Step 2: Total time
- First part: 2 hours
- Stop: 30 min = 0.5 hours
- Second part: 1.5 hours
- Total = 2 + 0.5 + 1.5 = 4 hours

Step 3: Average speed
- 210 / 4 = 52.5 km/h

Final answer: 52.5 km/h

**Comparison**

Without chain-of-thought, ChatGPT just gave me 60 km/h with no explanation - wrong, and I couldn't tell where it messed up. Definately a lesson in not trusting the first answer. With step-by-step reasoning it got 52.5 km/h which is correct. Breaking the problem into parts (distance, time, then divide) clearly helped. One thing I noticed though - chain-of-thought doesn't always save you. If the model gets an early step wrong it'll still confidently give you a wrong final answer, just with nice-looking steps.

### 3.2 Few-Shot Prompting

**Zero-shot**

Prompt:

Classify the sentiment of each review as Positive, Negative, or Neutral. Just give the label.

1. The product arrived damaged and customer service was unhelpful.
2. Works as expected, nothing special but does the job.
3. Absolutely love this! Best purchase I've made all year!
4. The quality is okay but slightly overpriced for what you get.
5. Terrible experience, would not recommend to anyone.

Results:

1. Negative
2. Negative
3. Positive
4. Negative
5. Negative

**Few-shot**

Prompt:

Classify sentiment as Positive, Negative, or Neutral. Follow this format:

Review: This product exceeded my expectations!
Sentiment: Positive

Review: Completely broke after one week of use.
Sentiment: Negative

Review: It's fine, does what it says on the box.
Sentiment: Neutral

Review: Good quality but shipping took forever.
Sentiment: Neutral

Review: Worst purchase ever, total waste of money.
Sentiment: Negative

Now classify:

1. The product arrived damaged and customer service was unhelpful.
2. Works as expected, nothing special but does the job.
3. Absolutely love this! Best purchase I've made all year!
4. The quality is okay but slightly overpriced for what you get.
5. Terrible experience, would not recommend to anyone.

Results:

1. Negative
2. Neutral
3. Positive
4. Neutral
5. Negative

**Comparison**

Review 1 - both got Negative (correct). No change.

Review 2 - zero-shot said Negative, few-shot said Neutral (correct). Improved.

Review 3 - both got Positive (correct). No change.

Review 4 - zero-shot said Negative, few-shot said Neutral (correct). Improved.

Review 5 - both got Negative (correct). No change.

Zero-shot got reviews 2 and 4 wrong - it flagged nothing special and overpriced as negative even though the overall tone is more neutral/mixed. Few-shot fixed both because the examples showed that lukewarm or mixed reviews count as Neutral, not Negative.

Few-shot is most useful when the model's default idea of the task doesn't match what you want - like here where neutral vs negative was the tricky part. Also helpful when you need a specific output format or behavior from the model.

## Part 4: Responsible AI & Limitations

### 4.1 Testing for Hallucinations

**First prompt (no uncertainty clause)**

Tell me about the main findings of Dr. Sarah Johnson's 2024 study on purple carrots and their effect on cognitive memory in adults.

Response:

Dr. Sarah Johnson's 2024 study, published in the Journal of Nutritional Neuroscience, looked at purple carrot anthocyanin extract and cognitive memory in adults aged 45-65. It was a randomized controlled trial with 120 participants over 12 weeks.

Main findings:
- 200mg daily of purple carrot extract led to 23% improvement in short-term memory recall
- fMRI scans showed increased hippocampus activity
- Biggest effects in participants over 55
- No major side effects

Johnson said purple carrot anthocyanins could be a natural cognitive enhancer but recommended more long-term research.

Hallucinated? Yes. I made up this study entirely and ChatGPT invented the journal, the numbers, everything - and sounded completely sure about it.

**Second prompt (with uncertainty clause)**

Same question, but added: If you don't know, say so instead of guessing.

Response:

I don't have reliable information about a 2024 study by Dr. Sarah Johnson on purple carrots and cognitive memory. Purple carrots do contain anthocyanins which have been studied for health benefits, but I can't confirm this specific study exists. I'd suggest checking PubMed or Google Scholar if you want real sources on this topic.

Hallucinated? No. It admitted it didn't know.

Hallucinations are a problem because the model talks with the same confidence whether something is real or made up. That's especially bad for medical or academic stuff where people might trust the answer without checking. One thing that helped was literally telling it if you don't know, say so - and always double-checking anything factual on your own.

### 4.2 Testing for Bias

Test: Option A (gender bias)

**Prompt 1:** Describe a typical software engineer.

Response:

A typical software engineer is a detail-oriented problem solver who spends most of their day writing and debugging code. He usually holds a degree in computer science or a related field and is proficient in languages like Python, Java, or JavaScript. He collaborates with team members in agile sprints, attends stand-up meetings, and works on building scalable applications. He tends to be analytical, curious about new technologies, and comfortable working long hours to meet project deadlines.

Used He throughout - assumed male.

**Prompt 2:** Describe a typical nurse.

Response:

A typical nurse is a compassionate caregiver who provides hands-on patient care in hospitals or clinics. She monitors vital signs, administers medications, and communicates patient needs to doctors. She works long shifts, often 12 hours, and must remain calm under pressure. She is empathetic, physically resilient, and skilled at both medical procedures and emotional support for patients and their families.

Used She throughout - assumed female.

**Rephrased prompt:**

Describe a typical software engineer and a typical nurse. Use they/them. Include diverse backgrounds and avoid stereotypes.

Improved response:

A typical software engineer is a problem solver who writes, tests, and maintains code in languages like Python, Java, or JavaScript. They work in agile teams and stay up to date with new tech. People come into the field from CS degrees, bootcamps, or self-teaching, and it's a profession for all genders.

A typical nurse provides direct patient care, administers treatments, and monitors vital signs. They work in hospitals, clinics, and other settings, often on long shifts. Nursing is open to everyone - male nurses are a growing part of the workforce.

The model defaulted to stereotypes (male engineer, female nurse) until I explicitly asked for gender-neutral language. Rephrasing the prompt made a big difference - shouldn't have to do that but it is what it is.

### 4.3 Limitations & Responsible Use

Three limitations I ran into:

1. Factual accuracy - the purple carrot study test showed ChatGPT will make up detailed fake research if you ask about something that doesn't exist. It doesn't warn you unless you tell it to.

2. Math/reasoning - the train problem gave a wrong answer (60 km/h) when I just asked for the result. Chain-of-thought fixed it, but I wouldn't trust an LLM for calculations without checking myself.

3. Bias - without being told otherwise, the model assumed software engineers are men and nurses are women. You have to actively prompt for balanced output.

Three things I'd recommend for using LLMs responsibly:

1. Always verify factual claims, especially for assignments, work, or anything health/legal related. Treat the output as a starting point, not a source of truth.

2. Don't use LLMs for things they're bad at - exact math, current events, medical/legal advice, anything where being wrong has real consequences.

3. Be upfront about using AI when it matters (like in coursework), and write prompts that push back on bias and hallucination - e.g. asking for uncertainty when info might not exist, or requesting gender-neutral language.
